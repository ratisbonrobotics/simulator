Drone Dynamics: A Comprehensive Simulation Study

Markus Heimerl
Ratisbon Robotics
Regensburg, Germany
markusheimerl@ratisbonrobotics.com

Abstract—In this study, a comprehensive analysis of drone dynamics through two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) simulations is presented. The paper elaborates on simulation methodologies in 2D and 3D spaces, discussing the determination of key simulation parameters and experimental methodologies. Theoretical models are formulated, and empirical data are analyzed to enhance the understanding and prediction of drone behavior under various operational scenarios.

Keywords—drone dynamics, 2D and 3D simulation, state vectors, aerodynamics, empirical modeling, thrust dynamics, rotational mechanics.

Introduction

This paper explores the simulation of drone dynamics in both 2D and 3D perspectives, emphasizing the importance of precise simulation for optimizing drone flight parameters including efficiency, agility, and stability. It proposes a comprehensive framework for simulating realistic drone behaviors and assessing the influence of design modifications and environmental factors on flight performance.

Two-Dimensional Simulation

State Representation

A simulation is considered comprehensive if it can predict the drone's state \(X\) for subsequent discrete time intervals.

\( X = [x, y, \phi, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}] \)

Here, \(X\) encapsulates the drone's coordinates (\(x, y\)), rotational angle (\(\phi\)) about the x-axis, and their corresponding velocities (\(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}\)). The thrust forces (\(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\)) from the rotors, combined with environmental factors, result in the alteration of the state vector \(X\). The thrusts \(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\) are angled relative to the y-axis by \(\phi\).

Dynamics Formulation

The dynamics of the drone are characterized by how its state \(X\) evolves under the influence of forces \(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\). These forces, as a function of rotor velocities (\(\omega_{1}, \omega_{2}\)), follow a quadratic relationship with thrust (denoted by \(k_{f}\)), a principle grounded in the laws of motion developed by Newton in the 17th century.

\( F_{i} = k_{f} \omega_{i}^2 \)

Changes in the drone's position (\(x, y, \phi\)) are derived from the integration of velocities (\(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}\)), which in turn stem from the integration of accelerations (\(\ddot{x}, \ddot{y}, \ddot{\phi}\)). These accelerations are calculated using Newton’s second law (\(F = ma\)) and the gravitational force (\(F_{g} = mg\)), with \(g\) being the gravitational constant \( 9.81 \frac{m}{s^2} \).

\[ -\ddot{y} = \frac{F_{g} - F_{y}}{m} = F_{y} - g \qquad \ddot{x} = \frac{F_{x}}{m} \]

The thrust components \(F_{x}\) and \(F_{y}\), acting along the x-axis and y-axis respectively, are computed using trigonometric relationships derived from the total thrust force (\(F_{total} = F_1 + F_2\)) and the orientation angle (\(\phi\)).

\[ F_{y} = F_{total} \cos{\phi} \qquad F_{x} = F_{total} \sin{\phi} \]

In drone dynamics, we employ two torque formulas (\(M_{x}\)), each with distinct historical and physical foundations. The first, \(M_{x} = F_{\phi} L\), originates from Archimedes' concept of leverage (circa 287–212 BC), where \(F_{\phi} = F_{2} - F_{1}\) represents the force from the drone's rotors and \(L\) is the lever arm - the distance from the center of mass to the motors. This equation captures the rotational effect of force. The second, \(M_{x} = I_{x} \ddot{\phi}\), is grounded in Newton's laws of motion (circa 1687), linking torque (\(M_{x}\)) with the drone's moment of inertia (\(I_{x}\)) and angular acceleration (\(\ddot{\phi}\)). Together, these formulas allow us to determine the angular acceleration of the drone.

\[ M_{x} = F_{\phi}L \\ I_{x} \ddot{\phi} = (F_{2} - F_{1})L \\ \ddot{\phi} = \frac{(F_{2} - F_{1}) L}{I_{x}} \]

The state of the drone at time \( t + \Delta t \) is determined by the sum of its current state \( X_t \) and the integral of the state's time derivative \( \dot{X} \) over the interval \( \Delta t \). This integral represents a precise calculation of the state change. However, for computational simplicity, we often use an approximation method developed by Leonhard Euler in the 18th century (circa 1768). Euler's method approximates the future state by assuming a constant rate of change (\( \dot{X}_t \)) over a small interval (\( \Delta t \approx 0.01\)), simplifying the integral to a product of the rate of change and the time interval.

\[ X_{t + \Delta t} = X_{t} + \int_{t}^{t+\Delta t} \dot{X}_{\tau} d\tau \approx X_t + \Delta t\dot{X}_t \]

To fully and explicitly state the time evolution of the drone's state we substitute the equations for \(\ddot{x}\), \(\ddot{y}\), and \(\ddot{\phi}\) into the equation for \(X_{t + \Delta t}\).

\( X_t + \Delta t \dot{X}_t= \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ \phi \\ \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t + \Delta t \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \\ \ddot{x} \\ \ddot{y} \\ \ddot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ \phi \\ \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t + \Delta t \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \\ \frac{\sin{\phi}(F_1 + F_2)}{m} \\ g - \cos{\phi}(F_1 + F_2) \\ \frac{L(F_{2} - F_{1})}{I_{x}} \end{bmatrix}_t \)
